- **Only approved companies can issue stablecoins.** You can't just launch a dollar-pegged token in the US anymore, you need to be a bank subsidiary, a federally approved nonbank issuer, or a state-approved issuer that meets similar standards.
- **Every stablecoin must be backed 1-to-1, with safe assets.** For every dollar of stablecoin issued, the issuer must hold a real dollar's worth of backing, cash, short-term Treasury bills (93 days or less), insured bank deposits, or similarly safe, liquid assets. No risky investments allowed as backing.
- **Reserves can't be reused or gambled with.** Issuers generally can't pledge, lend out, or reuse those backing reserves for other purposes, they have to sit there ready to cover redemptions, with only narrow exceptions.
- **Issuers must be transparent about their reserves.** They have to publish a monthly breakdown of exactly what's backing their coins, get it verified by an outside accounting firm, and have their CEO/CFO personally certify it's accurate, on penalty of criminal charges if they lie.
- **You can always redeem your stablecoin for real dollars.** Issuers must have a clear, public policy for letting people cash out, and they can't hide or randomly change fees; fee changes require 7 days' notice.
- **No paying interest on stablecoins.** Issuers are barred from paying holders interest or yield just for holding the coin, this is meant to keep stablecoins from functioning like unregulated bank accounts or investment products.
- **Stablecoins aren't government-backed, and issuers can't pretend they are.** It's illegal to imply a stablecoin is FDIC-insured, backed by the US government, or "legal tender." It's just a private company's IOU, backed by its own reserves.
- **Issuers get real bank-style oversight.** Depending on size and structure, issuers are supervised by federal banking regulators (the Fed, OCC, FDIC) or approved state regulators, with routine exams, anti-money-laundering rules, and sanctions compliance, just like banks already follow.
- **If an issuer goes bankrupt, stablecoin holders get paid first.** People holding the actual stablecoin have priority over other creditors, they get first claim on the reserves before anyone else in a bankruptcy.
- **Foreign stablecoins can be sold in the US only under strict conditions.** A non-US issuer (like a foreign competitor to USDC) can only operate here if their home country's regulations are officially recognized as comparable, they register with US regulators, and they hold enough US reserves to cover American customers, otherwise their coin can be banned from US platforms.
